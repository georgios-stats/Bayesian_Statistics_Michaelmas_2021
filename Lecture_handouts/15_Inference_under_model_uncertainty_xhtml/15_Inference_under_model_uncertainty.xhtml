1 Model uncertainty problem


Note 1. All (statistical/mathematical) models are approximations/simplifications of the reality. They are arguable. Their purpose is to allow You draw conclusions about reality and take decisions.
  • “All models are wrong, but some are useful”. –George Box's quote

Note 2. Let y=( y 1 ,..., y n ) be observables generated from the real but unknown data-generating process y ∼ R( y ) . Let z=( y n+1 ,..., y n+m ) be future outcomes from the same process R( ⋅ ) .

Note 3. 'Model uncertainty' is the problem where the statistician is uncertain about the proper way to formulate the statistical model M={F( y| θ ) ; θ ∈ Θ } in order to acceptably represent the data-generating process R( ⋅ ) .

Note 4. In problems under model uncertainty, often
  • the statistician initially considers a restricted set { M k ;k ∈ K } of available - reasonable - potential - competing - alternative statistical models M k ={ F k ( y| θ k ) ; θ k ∈ Θ k } ; then
  • interest lies in learning a model M which `best' represents R( y ) , performing (predictive or parametric) inference, and drawing conclusions under the pretense of model uncertainty.
Example 5.  [Variable selection in the Normal linear regression]. Consider a random quantity y ∈ Y ⊆ R which may depend on some of the variables of the set { φ 0 ,..., φ d-1 } . We are interested in learning the mapping M :
We restrict the mapping to those of the linear form y= ∑ j ∈ k φ j β j + ε with unknown sub-set of indexes k ⊆ {0,...,d-1} , unknown coefficients { β j } , and additive error ε I.I.D. according to the Normal distribution with zero mean and unknown variance σ 2 .
Unknowns are the sub-set k indexing the φ 's included in the mapping, associated coefficients { β j } , and variance σ 2 .
We collect observables { ( y i , φ i ) } i=1 n where ( y i , φ i ) is the i -th realization of vector ( y, φ 0 , φ 1 ,..., φ d-1 ) .

2 Standard framework

Note 6. Consider a collection of statistical models M ={ M k ;k ∈ K } , where each model M k ={ F k ( y| θ k ) ; θ k ∈ Θ k } , is labeled by an index k ∈ K , and associated with the sampling distribution F k ( y| θ k ) with pdf/pmf f k ( y| θ k ) , unknown parameter θ k ∈ Θ k , and parametric space Θ k .

Definition 7. The encompassing model M is defined as the collection of statistical models M ={ M k ;k ∈ K } ={ { F k ( y| θ k ) ; θ k ∈ Θ k } ;k ∈ K } labeled by parameter ϑ =( k, θ k ) ∈ varTheta which is defined on the joint parameter space varTheta = ∪ k ∈ K {k} × Θ k

Example 8. (Cont...) We specify encompassing model M ={ M k ;k ∈ K } as the collection of statistical models M k ={ y| β k , σ 2 ,k ∼ N ( Φ k β k ,I σ 2 ) ; β k ∈ R d k , σ 2 ∈ R + } , (1) where k ⊆ { 0,1,...,d-1 } is the label of the model indicating the sub-set of the regressors included in the statistical model M k . K is the collection of all sub-sets of { 0,1,...,d-1 } so that k ∈ K . Given M k , Φ k is the design matrix consisting of the regressors with indexes in k , β k ∈ R d k is the associated vector of regression coefficients, and d k is the number of regressors. Notice that σ 2 is kept to be common among all the models, however one could have set it to be different between different models (e.g.; σ k 2 ). The within model parameters are θ k =( β k , σ 2 ) defined on space Θ k = R d k × R . The parameter of M is ϑ =( k, β k , σ 2 ) and defined on space varTheta = ∪ k ∈ K {k} × R d k × R + .

Note 9. To complete the Bayesian model we specify prior distributions on the joint parametric space varTheta . Consider a collection of conditional priors on θ k given model M k as θ k | M k ∼ Π ( θ k | M k ) with pdf/pmf π ( θ k | M k ) ; and consider a marginal prior distribution on M k M k ∼ Π ( M k ) with pdf/pmf π ( M k ) , for all k ∈ K . The encompassing prior model has distribution Π ( M k , θ k ) with pdf/pmf π ( M k , θ k ) = π ( θ k | M k ) π ( M k ) , ∀ ( k, θ k ) ∈ varTheta

Note 10. Specifying the prior distribution Π ( M k , θ k ) is a delicate matter.
  1. Within model priors θ k | M k ∼ Π ( θ k | M k ) should not be improper because the normalizing constants are not canceled in the joint posterior (7), and hence the Lindley-Jeffreys padadox may kick in.
  2. If some models are embedded (nested) into others, e.g. M 1 ⊆ M 2 , possibly the marginal model prior should be π ( M 1 ) ≤ π ( M 2 ) , in order to be coherent –this is not a panacea.
  3. Common parameters between models (e.g. σ 2 in Example 8) may be treated as separate parameters. However, it is common to be regarded as the same parameter and be assigned the same prior; this is an additional approximation/simplification that modeler does for computational convenience –less parameters to learn ;-).
    • In Example 8, σ 2 is common to all models. If we wanted to be in accordance with this note, we should have specified σ 2 separately for each model M k , as σ k 2 , but we do not do this now (naughty...naughty).

Note 11. The full Bayesian model can be summarized as { y| θ k , M k ∼ F k ( y| θ ) , data generation fromt he sampling distribution θ k | M k ∼ Π ( θ k | M k ) , within model parameter generation from the conditional prior M k ∼ Π ( M k ) , model generation from the marginal model prior (2) hence the joint distribution is such that d P( y, θ k , M k ) = d F k ( y| θ ) d Π ( θ k | M k ) d Π ( M k ) .

Example 12. (Cont...) One can specify the following Bayesian linear regression model y| β k , σ 2 , M k ∼ N ( Φ k β k ,I σ 2 ) , the sampling distribution (3) β k | σ 2 , M k ∼ N ( μ k , σ 2 V k ) , conditional prior of the within model parameter (4) σ 2 | M k ∼ IG ( a, λ ) conditional prior of the within model parameter (5) M k ∼ π ( k ) = 1 | K | = 1 2 d , marginal model prior (6) Eq. 6 is the sampling distribution F( y| β k , σ 2 , M k ) for model M k . Eq. 6 and 6 are the conditional (or within) model priors Π ( β k , σ 2 | M k ) ; recall that they are conjugate to F( y| β k , σ 2 , M k ) and hence chosen for our computational convenience. Notice that parameter σ 2 , which is common to all available models { M k } , is treated as obtaining the same values among all models, and following the same prior. This is against the suggestion in Note 10(3.) but we assume that the computational benefits of reducing the dimensionality of the parametric space dominate the losses from the (possibly) worse approximation of the reality. In Eq. 6, the marginal model prior π ( k ) is chosen to be uniform across models (we have | K |= 2 d possible combinations or regressors).

Notation 13. To make the notation easier, we will denote M k as k and the probability measures as
  • F( y| θ k ,k ) := F k ( y| θ k ) and f( y| θ k ,k ) := f k ( y| θ k ) , as well as
  • Π ( θ k |k ) := Π ( θ k | M k ) , π ( θ k |k ) := π ( θ k | M k ) , Π ( k ) := Π ( M k ) , and Π ( k, θ k ) := Π ( M k , θ k ) , etc...

4 Inference based on a single Best model

For parametric or predictive inference, the statistician may proceed as follows:
  1. select a single 'Best' model M k * (optimal in some sense) from a set of available models M ={ M 1 , M 2 ,... } (Section 4.1) and then
  2. perform Bayesian inference conditional on the selected model M k * only and as if it had generated the data (Section 4.2); i.e. perform inference based on { y| θ k * , M k * ∼ F( y| θ k * , k * ) , data generation fromt he sampling distribution θ k * | M k * ∼ Π ( θ k * | k * ) , within model parameter generation from the conditional prior

4.1 Bayesian model selection: How to select the single 'Best' model M k * .

Note 21. Model selection/choice is the selection of the “Best” statistical model from a set of competing statistical models M ={ M k ;k ∈ K } . It can be naturally performed as a parametric point estimation problem, statistical decision problem, or hypothesis test (all equivalent). See Criteria 22 and 23.

Criterion 22. We produce the Bayes estimator (of Bayes rule) δ ˆ of parameter k ∈ K under a loss function ℓ ( δ ,k, θ k ) with decision space D = K and posterior distribution ( k, θ k ) ∼ Π ( k, θ k |y ) . I.e find δ ˆ : δ ˆ = arg min ∀ δ ∈ K E Π ( ℓ ( δ ,k, θ k ) | y . ) = arg min ∀ δ ∈ K ( ∫ Θ k ℓ ( δ ,k, θ k ) d Π ( k, θ k |y ) )

Criterion 23. Under the 0-1 loss ℓ ( δ ,k, θ k ) = 1 ( δ ≠ k ) the best model corresponds to the MAP δ ˆ that is model with the highest marginal model posterior probability π ( k|y ) , I.e δ ˆ such as: δ ˆ = arg max ∀ k ∈ K { π ( k|y ) } or equivalently δ ˆ such that π ( δ ˆ |y ) ≥ π ( k|y ) , for all k ∈ K .
Bayes factors can be used for model selection; here we re-form them suitably for the model selection framework.
Definition 24. Bayes factor B k,j ( y ) of model M k against model M j is defined as B k,j ( y ) = π ( k|y ) / π ( k ) π ( j|y ) / π ( j ) = f( y|k ) f( y|j ) ; ∀ k,j ∈ K

4.2 Within model inference

Note 26. Given that M k * is the selected “Best” model, interest may lie on learning the parameter θ k * ∈ Θ k * (or any function of it), or future outputs z=( y n+1 ,..., y n+m ) . Then inference is performed based on the Bayesian model { y| θ k * , M k * ∼ F( y| θ k * , k * ) , data generation from the sampling distribution θ k * | M k * ∼ Π ( θ k * | k * ) , within model parameter generation from the conditional prior
  • Parametric inference about θ k * (or functions of it) is performed based on the conditional posterior in (8): θ k * |y, k * ∼ Π ( θ k * |y, k * )
  • Predictive inference about a sequence of future outcomes z=( y n+1 ,..., y n+m ) is performed based on the conditional predictive distribution : z|y, k * ∼ G( z|y, k * ) in (10) with pdf/pmf g( z|y, k * ) = ∫ f( z|y, θ k * , k * ) d Π ( θ k * |y, k * )

5 Bayesian model averaging (BMA)

Note 27. Selecting a single (best) model from a set of available models and then proceeding as if the selected model had generated the observables may not be optimal. It ignores the uncertainty due to model selection, which leads to over-confident inferences and decisions. BMA offers a coherent mechanism to account model uncertainty.

Definition 28. Model averaging is called the process where a posterior summary is obtained as a weighted mean of the summaries under each model with weights given by the marginal model posterior probability.

Parametric inference
There may be certain model parameters or functions of model parameters which are present in all available models and retain a consistent interpretation across all models. Interesting may be to average out all the models.
Note 29. Let partition θ k =( ψ , φ k ) , where ψ ∈ Ψ is common in all models in M . It is possible to obtain the marginal posterior distribution Π ( ψ |y ) of ψ as a mixture of posterior distributions under each model with weights given by the marginal posterior probability of each model. i.e. Π ( ψ |y ) has pdf/pmf π ( ψ |y ) = ∑ k ∈ K π ( ψ |y,k ) π ( k|y ) ; with  π ( ψ |y,k ) = ∫ π ( ψ , φ k |y,k ) d φ k .

Note 30. Then any moment (expectation, variance, probability, etc...) of ψ ∈ Ψ can be expressed in the aforesaid weighted average form. Let h( ⋅ ) be a function defined on Ψ . Then E Π ( h( ψ ) |y ) = ∑ k ∈ K ∫ Θ k h( ψ ) d Π ( ψ |y,k ) π ( k|y ) = ∑ k ∈ K E Π ( h( ψ ) |y,k ) π ( k|y )

6 Discussion

If we think the model uncertainty concept broader, we may see that we often meet one of the following two situations:
M -close perspective:
the encompassing Bayesian model includes the real data-generation process R( ⋅ ) . It is argued that this is not a realistic situation. The introduced model uncertainty framework is suitable in this situation.
M -open perspective:
the encompassing Bayesian model does not include the real data-generation process R( ⋅ ) . This is a more realistic situation. It is argued that the introduced model uncertainty framework is not suitable in this situation in the sense that π ( M k ) does not make sense to exist.
What do we do? This is a rather open problem, and may depend on the application under consideration. Advise: Try to set-up an encompassing Bayesian model which is rich enough to include a model that acceptably approximates the real data generating process R( ⋅ ) or to include a combination of model able to construct an acceptable approximation of R( ⋅ ) for instance by BMA.
