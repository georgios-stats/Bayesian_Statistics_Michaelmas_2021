1 Sufficiency

It is often of interest to simplify the Bayesian model by reducing the complexity of the observables (data-set) y=( y 1 ,..., y n ) , where y ∈ Y . This can be for computational purposes: the data-set may involve large number of observations (large n ) or high-dimensional observables (high d ); or this can be for theoretical purposes: study how the information for the experiment affects the Bayesian model.
Key concepts to address data-set complexity are
  • Summary statistic (or statistic) aims to summarize part or all the relevant information supplied by the sample.
  • Sufficient statistic aims to summarize the whole of relevant information supplied by the sample.

1.1 Summary statistics

A statistic (or summary statistic) is a function of observations summarizing the main features of a sequence of observable quantities, y=( y 1 ,..., y n ) .
Definition 1. Let y=( y 1 ,..., y n ) be observable quantities such that y ∈ Y , and let function t: Y → R k with k ≤ n . The quantity t:=t( y ) is called a Statistic. The function t( ⋅ ) (or mapping t: Y → R k ) will be called statistic too.

Notation 2. Let T ={t:t=t( y ) , for some y ∈ Y } be the image of Y under t( ⋅ ) .

Notation 3. Let Y ( t ) ={y:t( y ) =t} is the set comprising all y 's such that the (the uncertain) t( ⋅ ) assumes value t . Essentially, t( ⋅ ) partitions the sample space Y into X t for all t ∈ T .
Note 4. The definition of statistic t( ⋅ ) as a function of the observable quantity y induces a probability distribution F( t| θ ) (of course it depends on the experiment e ∈ E as well but conditioning is omitted here) labeled by unknown parameter θ ∈ Θ , which is determined by sampling distribution F( y| θ ) . Given a prior distribution Π ( θ ) on θ , the posterior distribution Π ( θ |t ) of θ given t=t( y ) can be calculated from the Bayesian theorem. Hence: { t| θ ∼ F( t| θ ) θ ∼ Π ( θ )

Note 5. Sufficient statistic is a statistic that aims at summarizing the whole of relevant information supplied by the sample. We extend the concept of sufficiency and sufficient statistic (learned in SC2) to the Bayesian statistics.

Definition 9. The statistic t: Y → T is (parametric) sufficient for θ if and only if for any prior distribution Π ( θ ) with pdf/pmf π ( θ ) we get d Π ( θ |t=t' ) = d Π ( θ |y=y' ) , where t'=t( y' ) for some observed data y' and t'=t( y' ) . Both the quantity t and the function/mapping t( ⋅ ) , as well as their realizations/values, will be called sufficient statistics.

3 Likelihood principle

Consider two experiments e 1 and e 2 , one yielding data y 1 and the other yielding data y 2 . If the two likelihoods L 1 ( y 1 | θ ) and L 2 ( y 2 | θ ) are identical up to multiplication by arbitrary functions of y 1 or y 2 , then they contain identical information about θ and lead to identical posterior distributions. The experiments might be very different in other respects, but those differences are irrelevant for inference about θ .
Likelihood Principle
In making inferences or decisions about θ after y is observed, all relevant experimental information is contained in the likelihood function L( y| θ ) for the observed y . Furthermore, two likelihood functions contain the same information about θ if they are proportional to each other (as functions of θ ), e.g. L 1 ( y 1 | θ ) =c L 2 ( y 2 | θ ) for every θ ∈ Θ ; hence they must lead to identical inferences for θ .
Remark 29. Likelihood Principle:
  • ... implies that in order to draw any conclusion from an experiment only the actual observation matters and not the other possible outcomes that might have occurred but were not.
  • ... does not say that all information about θ is contained in likelihood function L( y| θ ) ; but just the experimental information. Other information relevant to the statistical analysis, such as prior information may exist.

Remark 30. Bayesian methods always satisfy the Likelihood principle. This is because, posterior knowledge about θ is expressed from the posterior distribution Π ( θ |y ) derived by the Bayesian theorem where all the knowledge regarding the experiment is expressed in the likelihood L( y| θ ) , and all the prior knowledge is expressed in the prior distribution Π ( θ ) exclusively.
Theorem 31. The likelihood principle is satisfied in the Bayesian framework.
Proof. Your belief about the uncertain parameter θ is represented by the posterior distribution. Consider two experiments e 1 and e 2 , one yielding data y 1 and the other yielding data y 2 , and assume that L 1 ( y 1 | θ ) =c L 2 ( y 2 | θ ) . Then if Π 1 ( θ | y 1 ) and Π 2 ( θ | y 2 ) have PDF/PMFs π 1 ( θ | y 1 ) and π 2 ( θ | y 2 ) : π ( θ | y 1 ) = L 1 ( y 1 | θ ) π ( θ ) ∫ Θ L 1 ( y 1 | θ ) d Π ( θ ) = c L 2 ( y 2 | θ ) π ( θ ) ∫ Θ c L 2 ( y 2 | θ ) d Π ( θ ) = π ( θ | y 2 )
